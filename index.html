Step-by-Step Signal Energy Analysis

Finding the energy for signals shown in the provided figure

Figure: Continuous-time signals for sets (a), (b), and (c)

Fundamental Formula

To find the energy of a continuous-time signal \( g(t) \), we use:

\[ E_g = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |g(t)|^2 dt \]

Note: Since these signals are rectangular pulses with amplitudes of \( 1 \) or \( -1 \), the squared magnitude \( |g(t)|^2 \) is always **1** over the duration of the signal and **0** elsewhere.

Set (a)

Signal \( x(t) \): A single pulse with amplitude 1 from \( t=0 \) to \( t=2 \).

\[ E_x = \int_{0}^{2} (1)^2 dt = [t]_0^2 = 2 \]

Signal \( y(t) \): Amplitude 1 from 0 to 1, and amplitude -1 from 1 to 2.

\[ E_y = \int_{0}^{1} (1)^2 dt + \int_{1}^{2} (-1)^2 dt = 1 + 1 = 2 \]
Result: E = 2

Set (b)

Signal \( x(t) \): A pulse of 1 from \( 0 \) to \( \pi \) and -1 from \( \pi \) to \( 2\pi \).

\[ E_x = \int_{0}^{\pi} (1)^2 dt + \int_{\pi}^{2\pi} (-1)^2 dt = \pi + \pi = 2\pi \]

Signal \( y(t) \): An oscillating square wave. Because \( |y(t)|^2 = 1 \) for the entire duration from \( 0 \) to \( 2\pi \):

\[ E_y = \int_{0}^{2\pi} 1 dt = 2\pi \]
Result: E = 2π

Set (c)

Signal \( x(t) \): Pulse of 1 from \( 0 \) to \( \pi/4 \) and -1 from \( \pi/4 \) to \( \pi \).

\[ E_x = \int_{0}^{\pi/4} (1)^2 dt + \int_{\pi/4}^{\pi} (-1)^2 dt = \frac{\pi}{4} + \left( \pi - \frac{\pi}{4} \right) = \pi \]

Signal \( y(t) \): A single pulse with amplitude 1 from \( 0 \) to \( \pi \).

\[ E_y = \int_{0}^{\pi} (1)^2 dt = \pi \]
Result: E = π
